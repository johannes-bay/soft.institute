Native Aotearoa Herbs for Wine

Research notes on tea infusions for pet-nat rectification — March 2026

Context: Proven combinations are Kawakawa + Sauvignon Blanc, and Kanuka bark/needles/wood + Chardonnay/Pinot Noir, always as pet-nat. Next blend: Pinot Noir / Pinot Gris light red with Sauvignon Blanc for sugar.

Tier 1 — Strong Candidates for Pinot Noir/Pinot Gris Light Red

Horopito Pseudowintera colorata

Recommended Leaf

Flavor: Hot peppery, earthy, slightly bitter, ginger-peppercorn warmth. The dominant compound is polygodial — a drimane sesquiterpene dialdehyde that causes pungency at concentrations as low as 0.1 μg.

Tannins & compounds: Tannins (astringent), eugenol, flavonoids (quercetin, kaempferol), phenolic acids, 29 essential oils. Eugenol provides analgesic and clove-like character.

Why it works for this blend: Pinot Noir already has lower tannin than other reds — horopito's tannins and peppery polygodial would add structural complexity and a warm spice finish that mirrors the varietal's natural spice notes. The earthiness complements Pinot Gris skin-contact character. Use sparingly — the heat intensifies.

Preparation: Dried leaf, crushed. Steep as tea then add to must. Start with low concentrations — polygodial is potent. A light infusion avoids overwhelming the fruit.

Koromiko Veronica stricta (formerly Hebe stricta)

Recommended Leaf tips / young buds

Flavor: Mild, slightly astringent, subtle banana note, soothing. The quintessential rongoa plant. Light enough to blend without dominating.

Tannins & compounds: High tannin content — the main active constituents are tannins that tighten tissues and have anti-inflammatory qualities. Also contains alkanes, mannitol, and phenolic acids.

Why it works for this blend: Koromiko's tannins would add structure and a drying finish to a light red that might otherwise lack body. The mild banana note is intriguing with Pinot Gris's pear/stone fruit character. Its astringency would help balance residual sugar from the Sauvignon Blanc component.

Preparation: Use only the unopened young leaf tips for best quality. Brew weak — a strong infusion is medicinally astringent. Blends well when combined with other herbs (traditionally mixed with kawakawa and manuka).

Manuka Leptospermum scoparium

Recommended Leaf / bark / wood

Flavor: Mild, earthy, floral, bitter (Captain Cook's "tea tree"). Aromatic and grounding.

Tannins & compounds: High in volatile oils, tannins, flavonoids, and triterpenes. Antibacterial, antifungal, antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory. The tannin and phenolic profile is robust.

Why it works for this blend: You already know kanuka works. Manuka is its close relative but earthier and more bitter with stronger tannin structure. The floral-earthy quality would complement Pinot Noir's mushroom/forest floor tertiary notes. Bark and wood would add oak-like structure without actual oak.

Preparation: Leaf for lighter floral notes, bark/wood for deeper tannic structure. Wood chips could be steeped like a tea or added during secondary fermentation for slow extraction.

Native Mint / Hioi Mentha cunninghamii

Recommended Leaf

Flavor: Clean, sweet minty fragrance. Lighter and more delicate than European mint. Prostrate creeping plant — very small leaves.

Tannins & compounds: Mint-family volatile oils. Used traditionally as a diaphoretic and aromatic herb.

Why it works for this blend: A small amount of hioi could add a fresh, lifting top-note to a light red/rose pet-nat — think of it as the herb equivalent of the green, herbal lift that Sauvignon Blanc already brings. Would help keep the wine feeling bright and drinkable rather than heavy. The sweetness complements residual sugar.

Preparation: Fresh or gently dried leaf. Use as a finishing herb in the tea blend — add late to preserve volatile aromatics. Very small quantities.

Tier 2 — Interesting Possibilities

Kumarahou Pomaderris kumeraho

Experiment carefully Leaf

Flavor: Bold, extremely bitter. Brews to a dark brownish-red. Traditionally sugar or peppermint was added to improve the taste. "Not for the faint-hearted."

Tannins & compounds: Flavonoids (quercetin, kaempferol), saponins, tannins, free amino acids, antioxidants. The high saponin content is notable — saponins are surface-active and create a "slippery" quality (gumdigger's soap).

Why it could work: The saponins are interesting for pet-nat — they could potentially affect mousse texture and bubble quality. The bitterness could provide an amaro-like backbone. The dark brownish-red brew color would deepen a light red. But saponin interaction with active fermentation needs testing.

Caution: High bitterness means very small doses. Saponins' effect on fermentation yeast is unknown — test in small batches first. Could cause foam issues during bottling.

Pikopiko Asplenium bulbiferum

Experiment carefully Young fronds only

Flavor: Green bean, asparagus, peppery celery. Earthy, nutty. Only 7 of ~200 NZ fern species are edible — mouku (hen and chickens fern) is the most widely eaten.

Tannins & compounds: High in vitamin E. Limited phytochemical data available.

Why it could work: The green, vegetal, asparagus-like quality is unusual for wine but could add a savory, umami dimension to a Pinot Noir blend — think of the green herb note in cool-climate Pinot. The "peppery celery" raw note would complement horopito's pepper.

Caution: Must be correctly identified — only 7 species are edible. Pick at 5-6 cm, before the koru unfurls. Once leaves open, the plant is inedible. Seasonal availability is limited.

Mairehau Leionema nudum

Experiment carefully Leaf (with caution)

Flavor: Highly aromatic, citrus-family (Rutaceae). Pleasant aromatic odour when crushed. Both flowers and foliage are scented — leaf fragrance comes from oil glands.

Tannins & compounds: Volatile oils from oil glands. Being in the Rutaceae (citrus) family, likely shares aromatic characteristics with citrus relatives.

Why it could work: The citrus aromatics could bridge between Sauvignon Blanc's citrus character and Pinot Noir's fruit. As an aromatic lift, even trace amounts could add complexity.

Caution: The leaf oil can cause photosensitive burns on skin exposed to strong sunlight. Internal safety data is limited — this is primarily used as a perfume plant, not a food plant. Test very conservatively.

Harakeke Flower Nectar Phormium tenax

Experiment carefully Flower nectar

Flavor: Sweet, delicious nectar. Traditionally collected in gourds as a natural sweetener. Flax flowers bloom June onwards.

Tannins & compounds: Natural sugars. Root contains different compounds (medicinal) — only the nectar is relevant here.

Why it could work: As a natural sugar source for the pet-nat's secondary fermentation rather than relying solely on Sauvignon Blanc for residual sugar. Would add a distinctly native terroir character to the sweetness itself. Seasonal — June flowering.

Caution: Harvesting enough nectar for winemaking quantities could be impractical. Fermentability of nectar sugars vs grape sugar needs testing.

Makomako / Wineberry Aristotelia serrata

Experiment carefully Berry

Flavor: Berry fruit — early settlers made wine and preserves from the berries, hence the common name "wineberry." Indigenous to Aotearoa, family Elaeocarpaceae.

Tannins & compounds: Berry pigments, natural sugars, fruit acids.

Why it could work: Historically used for wine. Could deepen the color and berry character of a light Pinot Noir red. A small addition of wineberry could push a rose toward a deeper light red with native fruit character.

Caution: Seasonal berry availability. Adding fruit to wine raises food safety questions around wild fermentation. Test small batches.

Tier 3 — More Speculative / Limited Use

Puha Sonchus oleraceus / S. kirkii (native)

Leaf / stem

Flavor: Bitter, salty, mildly astringent. The milky sap in the stem has strong bitterness. Traditionally rubbed under running water to reduce bitterness.

Wine potential: The bitterness and salinity could add a vermouth-like quality, but the strong bitter sap may be difficult to balance. More suited to a savory/culinary context than a fruit-forward pet-nat.

Mamaku / Black Tree Fern Cyathea medullaris

Pith / young shoots

Flavor: Turnip or marrow-like. Slimy when fresh, starchy after steaming. Traditionally a famine food — harvesting kills the tree.

Wine potential: The starch content could theoretically feed fermentation, but the flavor (turnip/marrow) is unlikely to complement wine. The ethical concern of killing a slow-growing tree fern makes this impractical.

Rata Nectar Metrosideros umbellata

Flower nectar

Flavor: Honey-like — rata honey is light-colored, with salty, caramel, silky buttery notes. Fruity aroma. One of the rarest NZ honeys.

Wine potential: Like harakeke nectar, interesting as a native sugar source. Rata honey's salty-caramel character could add complexity to a sweet component. But flowering is sporadic (good every 3 years, spectacular every 7) making supply unreliable.

Avoid

Kowhai Sophora spp.

Toxic — do not use

All parts contain cytisine, a toxic alkaloid. Seeds are particularly poisonous. Symptoms include nausea, vomiting, increased heart rate, muscle twitching, loss of coordination. Has killed cattle. Nectar has caused narcosis and death in honey bees. Human deaths from cytisine poisoning are documented.

Suggested Blends for Pinot Noir/Pinot Gris Light Red Pet-Nat

Blend Herbs Character
Spice & Structure Horopito leaf + Koromiko tips + Manuka bark Peppery warmth, tannic backbone, earthy depth. Would give the light red more body and a long, drying finish that counterpoints the Sauvignon sweetness.
Fresh & Aromatic Hioi (native mint) + Kawakawa + Koromiko tips Bright mint lift, pepper warmth, subtle banana. Light and drinkable — the herb character would sit alongside the fruit rather than replace it.
Forest Floor Manuka wood + Horopito leaf + Pikopiko fronds Deep, earthy, savory. The asparagus/green note from pikopiko with manuka's forest floor quality — plays to Pinot Noir's terroir-driven side.
Native Amaro Kumarahou leaf (tiny amount) + Horopito + Koromiko Bitter backbone, peppery mid-palate, astringent finish. Digestif character — the bitterness of kumarahou at low doses could add the kind of complexity you find in Italian amaro wines.

Wine Chemistry Notes

Tannin Interaction with Pet-Nat

Pinot Noir generally has lower concentrations of anthocyanins and tannins compared to other red varieties. Adding native plant tannins (especially from koromiko, horopito, and manuka) could compensate for this, adding structure without the heavy extraction of extended maceration. In a pet-nat context where there's active fermentation and residual sugar:

  • Plant tannins will bind with proteins and precipitate over time — additions are best made as tea (pre-dissolved) rather than raw plant matter
  • Residual sugar from the Sauvignon Blanc component will mask some astringency, so you can dose slightly higher than you might in a dry wine
  • CO2 in pet-nat accentuates perception of bitterness and tannin — factor this in when dosing horopito and kumarahou
  • Phenolic compounds from native plants may interact with anthocyanins from Pinot Noir/Pinot Gris — potential for color stabilization or shift

Saponins in Fermentation

Kumarahou's high saponin content could affect bubble formation and foam stability in pet-nat. Saponins are natural surfactants — they could theoretically create a finer, more persistent mousse. However, they may also interfere with yeast cell membranes during active fermentation. Test in small volumes first.

Food Safety

  • Stick to plants with established histories of internal use as tea/food (kawakawa, kanuka, manuka, koromiko, horopito, puha, pikopiko, hioi)
  • Avoid kowhai entirely (cytisine toxicity)
  • Mairehau has photosensitizing oils — limited internal safety data
  • Some rongoa plants are toxic and require trained preparation — when in doubt, consult rongoa practitioners
  • Wild-harvested plants should be correctly identified by someone experienced
  • Respect tikanga — offer karakia before harvesting, gather sustainably

Cultural Acknowledgement

These plants carry centuries of Maori knowledge — rongoa Maori was actively suppressed by the Tohunga Suppression Act (1907-1962) and is now experiencing a cultural resurgence. Using native plants in winemaking is an opportunity to honour this knowledge. Consider engaging with local rongoa practitioners and acknowledging the indigenous knowledge that makes this work possible.
